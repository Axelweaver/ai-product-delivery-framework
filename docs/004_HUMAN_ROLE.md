# Introduction

The AI Product Delivery Framework is built around one fundamental assumption:

**Artificial Intelligence assists product delivery.  
Humans remain responsible for the product.**

Regardless of how capable AI systems become, they do not own business objectives, legal responsibility, financial risk or long-term vision.

The human remains the central decision maker throughout the entire product lifecycle.

This document defines the responsibilities, authority and expectations of the human participant within the framework.

---

# Human-Centered Product Delivery

The framework is intentionally **human-centered**, not AI-centered.

AI increases productivity.

AI improves consistency.

AI accelerates delivery.

However, AI does not replace ownership.

The framework assumes that every project has at least one human who owns the product.

Depending on the organization, this may be:

- Founder
- Product Owner
- Entrepreneur
- Team Lead
- Engineering Manager
- Project Manager
- Technical Lead
- Solo Developer

Titles may differ.

Responsibility does not.

---

# Human Responsibilities

The human owns decisions that affect the future of the product.

These responsibilities cannot be delegated to AI.

They include:

- defining product vision;
- defining business goals;
- approving product principles;
- approving architecture;
- prioritizing work;
- accepting risks;
- approving releases;
- making financial decisions;
- defining success criteria.

AI may advise.

Only the human decides.

---

# Human Authority

The human has final authority over:

- product direction;
- roadmap;
- MVP scope;
- architecture acceptance;
- implementation approval;
- documentation approval;
- release approval;
- budget allocation;
- technology adoption;
- AI provider selection.

Framework rule:

> AI may recommend any decision.

> AI may never make the final decision.

---

# Human Accountability

The human remains accountable for:

- product success;
- business outcomes;
- customer satisfaction;
- legal compliance;
- financial performance;
- team organization;
- project quality.

AI cannot accept responsibility.

Therefore AI cannot own responsibility.

---

# AI Is Not A Replacement

The framework explicitly rejects the idea that AI replaces experienced professionals.

Instead:

AI amplifies expertise.

An experienced engineer becomes more productive.

An experienced product owner makes better-informed decisions.

An experienced architect explores more alternatives.

Likewise:

Poor decisions remain poor decisions even if AI generates them faster.

The framework assumes continuous growth of human competencies.

Important competencies include:

## Product Thinking

Understanding:

- customer problems;
- market needs;
- user value;
- prioritization;
- validation.

## Systems Thinking

Understanding:

Ability to question:

- AI output;

- AI agents;
- developers;

- customers.

## Decision Making

One of the most valuable human skills.

Humans choose.

# Human And Multiple AI Systems

The framework encourages using multiple AI systems.

Different systems often have different strengths.

Example:

- ChatGPT for strategic thinking.
- Claude Code for implementation.
- Gemini for research.
- Another AI for independent review.

The human coordinates all participants.

The human integrates their outputs.

The human resolves conflicts.

---

# Resolving AI Disagreements

Different AI systems will sometimes recommend different solutions.

This is expected.

It should not be treated as a problem.

Instead, disagreement creates opportunities for better decisions.

Recommended process:

1. Identify the disagreement.
2. Understand assumptions.
3. Compare trade-offs.
4. Review project context.
5. Make a documented decision.

Whenever possible, record important decisions in the repository.

---

# Human Review Checkpoints

The framework recommends explicit approval gates.

Typical checkpoints include:

- Product Discovery completed.
- Product documentation approved.
- Architecture approved.
- Task decomposition approved.
- MVP scope approved.
- Release approved.

AI prepares.

Human approves.

---

# When The Human Should Stop AI

Sometimes AI should not continue.

Examples include:

- conflicting product goals;
- missing business requirements;
- unclear architecture;
- inconsistent documentation;
- unresolved legal questions;
- contradictory review reports.

In these situations:

Stop.

Clarify.

Then continue.

Continuing with incorrect assumptions only increases future cost.

---

# Delegating Work To AI

Delegation is encouraged.

Blind delegation is not.

Before assigning work, the human should define:

- expected outcome;
- available context;
- constraints;
- success criteria;
- review requirements.

Clear delegation produces better results.

---

# What Can Be Delegated

Typical AI responsibilities include:

- documentation;
- implementation;
- code review;
- research;
- test generation;
- architecture proposals;
- migration analysis;
- report generation;
- roadmap suggestions.

Delegation saves time.

Responsibility remains human.
